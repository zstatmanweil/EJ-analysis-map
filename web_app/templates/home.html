Introduction

Understanding potential disparities in communities’ compliance with the Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA) will help managers effectively and fairly allocate funding for improving drinking water systems. This study investigated the relationship between socioeconomic status and race, and violations to the SDWA by community water system (CWS) in the state of New Jersey between 2012 and 2016. This research was a continuation of my master's thesis, which focused on Pennsylvania.

Spatial Analysis

The sociodemographic characteristics of the water systems were estimated using one CWS-level spatial analysis method (areal weighting) and a county-level spatial analysis method:

  • Areal Weighting This method assigns a proportion of the population to the affected area relative to the percent of the geographic unit within the discrete affected area boundary (e.g., a buffer). This method assumes equal distribution of the population within the census tracts.
  • County-level Analysis This method assigned the percent people of color and the percent below the poverty line of the CWS’s county to that CWS. Although some CWSs serve more than one county, the primary one was determined based on the county listed in the SDWIS database.
The demographics estimated from these spatial analysis methods are shown in the map.

Statistical Analysis

Negative binomial regression was then applied to determine if these sociodemographic characteristics, and other water system variables (rural vs urban, public vs private, small vs large, groundwater vs surface waterr), are associated with the number of total and/or health-based SDWA violations.
